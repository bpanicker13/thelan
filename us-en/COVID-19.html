COVID-19 and Work-remote for government

How to thrive in the future of work.
  • by: Binu Panicker | May 7, 2020

F For government agencies, COVID-19 has made the future of work a current workplace reality. Learn how leaders can act now to embrace the transition and get ready for what is to come next.

For government agencies the rapid and abrupt transition to remote work triggered by covid-19 has not been completely smooth. Some feel that they lack the facilities and protection needed to accommodate so many people working from home. Some companies are experimenting with shift-based working hours in order to conform to social distancing strategies, while security experts are worried about the increased attack surface being accessed at home with confidential government information.

But after the pandemic is over, it won't be that easy to move from remote back into the physical workplace. When the U.S. Social Security Administration implemented the second phase of a plan in March to end a decades-old telework system and reintegrate workers into full-time, office-based work, workers opposed the move, employees became relaxed and happier working from home.

In a 2018 survey, 76% of public and private sector employees indicated they would be more loyal to their employers if they had flexible work options.4

The Office of Personnel Management’s 2018 Report on the status of telework in the federal government found that 63% of respondents believed telework improved their performance.7

Given that both public sector organizations tend to have workers employed at home in reaction to the COVID-19 pandemic, they could be experiencing a transition that they cannot quickly take back. Workflows are likely to have to adapt, and labor must settle into a new way of life. When returning to the office is safe, workers might not be going back to the same job. In certain situations, may have modified the complexity of their work and goals. Remote work could not only be an annoyance in the short term. It seems like we might be on the cusp of a long-term transition. COVID-19 has sped up the timetable for future study

We are on the cusp of a long-term transformation. COVID-19 has accelerated the timeline for the work of the 21st century—it will revolutionize all aspects of how we work, where we work, and what efficiencies teams can build together.

What does that mean for government agencies? It means teams need to consider adapting to today's new normal. Meanwhile, leaders and politicians need to plan for tomorrow's "next standard," collecting the data they need to recognize what works well and what doesn't, so they can make strategic decisions that both improve and retain the employee experience.

Government agencies in today's transition are dealing with a variety of challenges and many are tactical and technical. High bandwidth allowing for increased personnel access to the same VPN. There is a rise in cyber threats and more people working remotely can reveal vulnerabilities that hackers can exploit. The Department of Defense alone is planning to spend $300 million to upgrade its technical infrastructure.

Government agencies in today's transition are dealing with a variety of challenges and many are tactical and technical. High bandwidth allowing for increased personnel access to the same VPN. There is a rise in cyber threats and more people working remotely can reveal vulnerabilities that hackers can exploit. The Department of Defense alone is planning to spend $300 million to upgrade its technical infrastructure.

But continuity of operations is not just about technology — it is about the people behind the screens who must interact with machines, as well as other humans, to get things done. There are aspects of the "modern standard" to pay attention to as we adjust rapidly to today's virtual environment and learn to communicate, handle, and execute.

Outcomes, process, and schedules—and the skills you need to achieve them—can be built with efficiencies. The issue today is not what job can be performed virtually. It is how the members of organizations can better handle the virtual version of nearly all the work. Government agency managers are navigating an unprecedented challenge — learning how to handle it virtually, while learning how to work with their teams differently.

Federal employees are now recognizing that people who work from mobile or remote devices need new technology techniques and training. In a virtual world, just delivering presentations and engaging with an audience is a new skill. Managers must be mindful that certain goals can be harder to achieve for both themselves as well as team leaders. It may seem simple, but it cannot be overestimated: some of the tasks you need to perform during this period will become more complicated.

Timelines will need to be modified to meet the demands of remote teamwork, or to satisfy the need for physical access to data or systems; organizations may need to set shift schedules to implement social distancing practices. To reduce the number of people entering the workplace to access classified information, the DoD has classified its workers according to three categories of remote access needs.13 "Essential" employees with jobs that cannot be performed remotely have needed new safety protocols and health standards.

Embracing Workforce Virtualization

Most leaders are probably too concerned with those immediate changes to consider how today's virtualization will affect us tomorrow, but now is the time to think about it. This transition is likely to occur right around the time everyone gets used to working from home and appreciates some of the flexibility and work-life balance it can offer.

We don’t expect that the end of the pandemic will bring us all back into our offices to “business as usual,” as if nothing had happened.

A 2019 study found that “virtual teams benefited from high levels of task interdependence because it required them to interact with each other more frequently and gain an understanding of one another, such as by asking questions or seeking feedback.”15

The uncertainty around COVID-19 presents an opportunity for agency leaders to be proactive, and not just about expanding the use of collaboration tools or enhancing IT infrastructure and capacity. It’s an opportunity to engage in real-time learning about how you’re working today, and data-backed decision-making about how you want to work in the future.

Right now, agency leaders can establish mechanisms to capture and aggregate data on which tasks are being performed the same, better, or worse than before the transition to virtualization, across jobs, teams, and functions. This data needs to come from leaders and managers, and from teams: What challenges are they facing? What’s better and worse about working from home? Which tasks are truly mission-critical? How can the work be deconstructed to get granular about those truly mission-critical activities?

Starting to gather data now will not only help agencies improve over the next few months, it will also help establish the building blocks for the right strategy in the months and years to come. Leaders can establish and employ metrics to track productivity and outcomes as much as it makes sense to, but they shouldn’t discount the importance of employee perspective, preferences, and experience. Staying authentic means recognizing the limits of data. They can encourage employees to capture their thoughts on the work-from-home experience via anonymous surveys, interviews, focus groups, or using unique tools, such as journaling.

Agency leaders need to attract talent with a data-centric strategy around a virtual workplace.

After the data has been gathered, leaders can do rapid scenario planning and build a post-COVID workplace strategy that accounts for the considerations and factors we’ve discussed in this article. This should start with defining the “workplace” as more than just the physical office location. Now more than ever, the workplace is the combination of where people work along with the culture, collaboration tools, and proximity choices needed to enable the most productive work and the best workforce experience.

Agency leaders can use the forced situation of having to work at home in response to COVID-19 as an opportunity to get better for the future.

By assessing, prioritizing, and developing strategies in real time to match work activities to the optimal workplaces, leaders can help improve and rethink the work of government agencies for years to come.

Today, as agencies rapidly “virtualize,” they can also learn from the bumps in the road, so that when teams come back together in person, they know how to work together, better.
